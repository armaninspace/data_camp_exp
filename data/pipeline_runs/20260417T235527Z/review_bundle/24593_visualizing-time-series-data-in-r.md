## Visible Curated Q/A Pairs

Q: How would I know when a univariate view is not enough for the question I want to answer?  
A: A univariate view is not enough when your question involves relationships or patterns between multiple variables, or when you need to understand how different series interact. If you only look at one variable, you may miss important context or dependencies that are only visible when comparing multiple series together.

Q: What is the difference between a univariate and a multivariate time series?  
A: A univariate time series involves a single variable measured over time, focusing on its distribution, central tendency, and spread. A multivariate time series involves two or more variables measured over time, allowing you to analyze patterns and relationships between them.

Q: How would I know that I am missing an important relationship by only looking at one series at a time?  
A: You might be missing an important relationship if patterns, trends, or changes in one series are related to or influenced by another series. If your analysis only considers one series at a time, you could overlook interactions or dependencies that are only apparent when examining multiple series together.

Univariate Time Series

Univariate plots are designed to learn as much as possible about the
distribution, central tendency and spread of the data at hand. In this
chapter you will be presented with some visual tools used to diagnose
univariate times series.

Multivariate Time Series

What to do if you have to deal with multivariate time series? In this
chapter, you will learn how to identify patterns in the distribution,
central tendency and spread over pairs or groups of data.
